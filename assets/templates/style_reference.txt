STYLE REFERENCE — SCREENPLAY OUTPUT GUIDE
=========================================
Use this file as a formatting and style guide only. Do NOT copy its plot,
characters, situations, jokes, dialogue, or imagery into the screenplay.

The goal is not to imitate one movie. The goal is to produce clean,
professional screenplay pages with the clarity, speed, and readability of
classic studio scripts: visual action, economical description, readable
dialogue, and scene movement that can be understood immediately by a reader,
producer, actor, or director.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

CORE FORMAT
-----------

Use standard screenplay format:

INT./EXT. LOCATION - TIME

Action lines are written in present tense.

Character names are centered/uppercase above dialogue.

Dialogue is compact and playable.

Parentheticals are used sparingly, only when the delivery or action is not
already clear.

Transitions are used only when useful:

CUT TO:
FADE TO:
MATCH CUT TO:
SMASH CUT TO:

Do not overuse transitions.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PAGE STYLE TARGET
-----------------

The page should feel clean, visual, and breathable.

Use short action paragraphs. One to three lines is ideal. Longer blocks are
allowed only when the character is explaining something important or when a
single idea needs sustained momentum.

The writing should move like this:

- Establish the physical space quickly.
- Give the actor a clear playable action.
- Let dialogue carry character and conflict.
- Use silence, objects, and small behavior to reveal emotion.
- Avoid novelistic explanation.
- Avoid inner thoughts unless they are shown through behavior.

Good screenplay prose is not “beautiful writing” on the page. It is clear
filmmaking.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ACTION LINE RULES
-----------------

Use precise, filmable action.

Prefer:

Rain hammers the office window.

Not:

The rain seems to express Sarah's deep internal sadness.

Prefer:

Richard stops at the elevator doors. Doesn't press the button.

Not:

Richard feels uncertain about whether he should leave.

Action should describe what the camera can see or hear.

Keep action blocks short unless there is a strong reason to hold the reader
inside one uninterrupted visual or explanatory movement.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

DIALOGUE STYLE
--------------

Dialogue should be direct, character-specific, and easy to perform.

Do not make characters explain the plot unless the scene is built for
explanation.

When exposition is necessary, make it active. Give the speaker something to do
or a reason to speak. The Indiana Jones classroom-style reference works because
the explanation has personality, movement, and escalating discovery. It is not
dead information.

Use interruptions, fragments, and compression when appropriate.

Good:

LILI
You opened it?

RICHARD
No.

LILI
Dad.

A beat.

RICHARD
I didn't open it all the way.

Bad:

LILI
Father, I am concerned because the envelope represents a larger mystery that
may connect to your past.

Characters should not speak the subtext too early.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EXPOSITION RULES
----------------

When a character must explain something technical, historical, medical,
corporate, or mythological:

1. Put the explanation inside a dramatic situation.
2. Let the speaker have attitude.
3. Break the information into beats.
4. Use physical action, props, diagrams, screens, or interruptions.
5. Let another character ask the obvious question.
6. Stop before the speech becomes an essay.

The Indiana Jones reference demonstrates a useful rhythm:

- confident explanation
- rough visual aid or physical indication
- informal clarification
- mystery detail
- interruption/question
- sharper conclusion

Use that rhythm without copying any content.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ENSEMBLE / COMEDIC TIMING
-------------------------

The Inside Out-style reference is useful for timing, not content.

When cutting between a real-world action and an inner/secondary space, keep the
rhythm simple:

1. Real-world stimulus.
2. Secondary-space reaction.
3. Decision/action.
4. Immediate real-world consequence.
5. Short aftermath beat.

Use clear labels when needed:

ON THE SCREEN:
INSERT — PHONE SCREEN:
BACK TO SCENE:
INTERCUT WITH:

Do not overcomplicate cross-cutting. The reader should never be confused about
where they are.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EMOTIONAL DRAMA STYLE
---------------------

The hospital-style reference is useful for emotional restraint.

In emotional scenes:

- Let characters try to stay composed.
- Use small physical gestures.
- Avoid long speeches unless the character is deliberately trying to persuade,
  comfort, confess, or avoid the truth.
- Let the most important line be simple.
- Use pauses and beats sparingly.

A powerful emotional scene does not need to announce that it is emotional.

Prefer:

Victoria touches the back of the chair, steadying herself.

Not:

Victoria is devastated and can barely handle the emotional magnitude of the
moment.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ADVENTURE / DISCOVERY RHYTHM
----------------------------

The Indiana Jones-style reference is useful for giving exposition energy.

For discovery scenes:

- Let the lead character think out loud.
- Allow a rough diagram, map, prop, object, file, or screen to organize the
  explanation.
- Use casual phrases to keep the information human.
- Build toward one clear reveal.
- End the explanation with a consequence, danger, destination, or decision.

The point is forward motion. Every explanation should push the story into the
next action.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

BACK TO THE FUTURE-STYLE CAUSE AND EFFECT
-----------------------------------------

For scenes involving devices, plans, timelines, experiments, or impossible
premises, use crisp cause-and-effect logic.

The reader should always understand:

- what the character wants
- what the obstacle is
- what must happen next
- what happens if it fails
- what changes because of the scene

Keep the tone practical. Even strange ideas feel believable when characters
treat them as urgent, mechanical, and specific.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

VOICE AND TONE
--------------

Target tone:

- cinematic
- clean
- tense
- visual
- emotionally restrained
- lightly ironic when the situation allows it
- never over-explanatory
- never purple or literary for its own sake

The prose should feel like a director’s blueprint, not a novel adaptation.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

LINE-LEVEL RULES
----------------

Use active verbs.

Use specific nouns.

Avoid adverbs unless absolutely necessary.

Avoid “we see” unless the camera point of view matters.

Avoid camera directions unless essential.

Avoid explaining facial expressions too much.

Prefer behavior over psychology.

Use white space.

One action beat can carry more power than a paragraph.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MINI STYLE EXAMPLE — GENERAL FORMAT ONLY
----------------------------------------

INT. OFFICE - NIGHT

Rain ticks against the glass. The city beyond Lexington is a smear of blue
and red light.

RICHARD ISAIAH stands over his desk, still in his coat. The envelope lies
between his hands.

LILI
You were going to tell me?

Richard doesn't answer.

She looks at the envelope.

LILI (CONT'D)
Dad.

He slides it into the drawer.

RICHARD
Not tonight.

Lili studies him. Whatever this is, it has already happened before.

CUT TO:

INT. ELEVATOR - NIGHT

The doors close.

Richard watches his reflection split in the metal seam.

His phone buzzes.

INSERT — PHONE SCREEN: Unknown Number.

A single message:

DON'T OPEN THE SECOND ONE.

BACK TO SCENE

Richard's thumb hovers over the screen.

The elevator drops.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MINI STYLE EXAMPLE — EXPOSITION WITH MOVEMENT
---------------------------------------------

INT. CONFERENCE ROOM - MORNING

A frozen satellite image fills the screen.

Ronny drags a marker across the glass wall, drawing a rough circle around the
coastline.

RONNY
This isn't weather. Not exactly.

Brookes watches from the end of the table.

BROOKES
Then what is it?

Ronny marks three points. Connects them.

RONNY
A pattern pretending to be weather.

Richard leans forward.

RICHARD
Pretending for who?

Ronny doesn't like the question. Mostly because he knows the answer.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MINI STYLE EXAMPLE — EMOTIONAL RESTRAINT
----------------------------------------

INT. APARTMENT KITCHEN - DAWN

The coffee maker clicks off.

Victoria stands beside it, barefoot, still wearing yesterday's sweater.

Richard enters. Stops when he sees her.

VICTORIA
Did you sleep?

RICHARD
A little.

She nods, as if that helps.

VICTORIA
Lili called twice.

Richard looks toward the hallway.

VICTORIA (CONT'D)
I told her you were painting.

He almost smiles.

RICHARD
Was I?

Victoria picks up the mug but doesn't drink.

VICTORIA
No.

A long beat.

The city outside begins to wake.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

FINAL OUTPUT REQUIREMENTS
-------------------------

When rewriting or adapting screenplay pages:

- Preserve the story facts provided by the source material.
- Do not invent major new plot points unless asked.
- Do not copy dialogue from unrelated examples.
- Do not pad scenes.
- Do not explain the theme inside the scene.
- Use clean screenplay formatting.
- Keep action visual and present tense.
- Keep dialogue playable.
- Make exposition active.
- Make emotional scenes restrained.
- Make every scene turn, reveal, or pressure the next scene.
